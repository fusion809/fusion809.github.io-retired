Date Significance Pros as Australia Day Cons as Australia Day
1 January Date of Federation. Birth of the nation, perhaps our single most important date. Climate is also similar to that of the existing Australia Day, so may cause fewer people to dislike the change. Also New Year’s Day, an existing holiday that is likely to overshadow the fact it’s Federation Day. Many people are also intoxicated on this day from the previous night’s festivities, so this might also hamper the celebration of the day.
19 January Written as 19.01, the year of Federation. Alternative way of celebrating Federation, without clashing with an existing holiday. Also has a similar climate to 26 January. Not the actual date of any significant event.
26 January Arrival of the First Fleet. Existing holiday preferred by many people, especially those of predominantly British descent. It is the date that Australia as we know it started. Also the date indigenous Australians started to be abused and oppressed by the Europeans, hence a day of mourning for many indigenous Australians.
13 February Official date of apology to the Stolen Generations by PM Kevin Rudd in 2008. An important date of reconciliation. It has a similar climate to that of 26 January, which might be desirable in minimizing the unpleasantness associated with changing the date. Mostly only of significance to indigenous Australians. I realize that this is a date of reconciliation that should be significant to all Australians, but most non-indigenous Australians don't care that much about that date.
3 March Commencement date of the Australia Acts 1986. The latest possible date we attained our legislative independence from the UK. As it required cooperation between the UK, Commonwealth and state parliaments, it also marks a date of intergovernmental cooperation. Similar climatic conditions to that of 26 January. Some people that love the UK see it as a day of mourning, as it’s the day we declared we could make it on our own and severed our last legislative link to the UK.
8 May May 8 sounds like mate. Can be used to signal mateship, which could be argued to be a key Australian value. Uses an American date format and doesn’t actually commemorate any important date. Climate is also markedly different to that of the existing Australia Day.
9 May Opening date of the first Federal Parliament in 1901, Old Parliament House in 1927, and New Parliament House in 1988. An important date on three related fronts─date of the opening of the first Federal Parliament, opening of Old Parliament House and opening of New Parliament House. The first Federal Parliament was in Victoria, so could be seen as divisive between the states, but the subsequent parliament buildings were in Canberra, so it shouldn't be. A fairly politically significant day, most Australians don’t care about politics and hence it may be a hard day to celebrate. Climate is also significantly different to that of the existing Australia Day.
26 May Sorry Day, the date that in 1998 PM John Howard apologized to indigenous Australians for the government’s historic mistreatment of them, specifically the Stolen Generations. An important date of reconciliation. It is a day that is predominantly of significance to a particular demographic of Australians, hence could be argued as unsuitable as Australia Day, which is meant to be a day of celebration for everyone. Climate may also be an issue.
27 May Anniversary of the 1967 referendum on indigenous rights. Also the date of Sir Henry Parkes’ birthday. Celebrates a landmark day in the progression of indigenous rights and the birth date of Australia’s ‘Father of Federation’, Sir Henry Parkes. As it marks two significant events, one of indigenous significance and the other of more general Australian significance, it could have greater power to unite us than other indigenous-centric dates. Climate is significantly different to that of the existing Australia Day.
3 June Anniversary of the Mabo Decision in 1992. Very important date of indigenous significance. Mostly just of indigenous significance, also has weather quite different to that of the existing date for Australia Day.
18 June Anniversary of when, in 1962, indigenous Australians, regardless of their prior military service, gained the legal right to vote in federal elections. An important date for civil rights. While an important date, indigenous Australians did not have fully equal voting rights back then. Compulsory enrolment, for example, did not exist on the federal level. Also it is in winter, so the climate is markedly different to that of the existing Australia Day.
9 July Constitution Day, the date Queen Victoria gave royal assent to the Constitution Act. Federation is an event that few have negative opinions of. It’s a date we can celebrate what we have in common—a nation, a Constitution, and a history that belongs to all of us. Could also be an opportunity to increase awareness of the Constitution, given that many Australians aren't even aware we have a Constitution to begin with, let alone that a referendum is required for any amendment to it to be made. Almost in the middle of winter, so climate is as different to that of the existing Australia Day as it can get. Some republicans see it as a date of little significance, as Federation did not give us independence from the UK, hence they argue we weren't really a nation at the time of Federation. As a counterargument, I, a republican, see it as a date of significance, as it was the date that a Constitution that the members of the various Australian colonies (to be fair, WA didn't until after the Constitution was enacted) democratically (by the standards of the day, although universal adult suffrage was only present in South Australia) agreed to was enacted and while we didn't gain complete independence, the High Court in Sue v Hill did argue it was at least a milestone in our evolution of independence.
1 September Wattle Day. First day of spring; an existing date of Australian significance. Coldness of winter is usually still present, so its climate is still markedly different to that of the existing Australia Day.
9 October Date the Statute of Westminster Adoption Act 1942 commenced, although retroactively applied back to 3 September 1939 (the start of WWII). Date, at the very latest, the federal government attained its legislative independence from the UK, although the states may have still been linked to the UK. Was an event that only really occurred as a means of preventing the UK from legally overriding our wartime decisions. Given the circumstances, celebrating this date could be seen as celebrating a war that triggered us to sever this link. Some people with more affection for the UK than most might mourn this date as the day we severed the federal government's legislative ties to the UK.
24 October Date of the Tenterfield Oration in 1889. (Arguably) the date the chain of events that lead to Federation began. Also a day we can celebrate a man that personifies many of the things we cherish as a nation. Such as the ability of someone that came from humble beginnings and even another country to rise to the top position in our country through hard work and determination. Not the first time Federation had been proposed, although it was the time people paid the most attention to.
11 November Date of the death of Ned Kelly, a notable figure in Australia’s history; Remembrance Day; and the date in 1975 that our Constitution triumphed over its greatest ever test. Celebrates the power of our Constitution and its ability to triumph over adversity. Also allows people the day off to fully commemorate Remembrance Day. Celebrating on a date of respectful and quiet commemoration of the sacrifice of our veterans (Remembrance Day) could be seen as offensive. Also potentially offensive to Labor Party and Gough Whitlam supporters and those with positive views of Ned Kelly to celebrate this date. I'm an admirer of Gough Whitlam too, but I do not find celebrating/commemorating the date offensive, at least not on that basis.
3 December Eureka Stockade Day Arguably the single most important date in the history of Australian democracy. Some view it as a purely Victorian event.
Legend:
  1. Those days with a cyan background are those of general constitutional significance.

  3. The blue background is for days of general democratic significance.
  4. The red background is for days of indigenous significance.
  5. The yellow background is for other days.
